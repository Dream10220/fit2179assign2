01   Overview
01The Big Picture

What's at stake?

Australia harbours more unique species than almost anywhere on Earth. But that richness is under threat. Using data from the Atlas of Living Australia, the EPBC Act, and the national protected areas database, this page traces where threatened species live, which groups face the greatest danger, and whether conservation effort is keeping pace.

What is the EPBC Act? The Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 is Australia's main environmental law. It classifies species as Vulnerable, Endangered, Critically Endangered, or Extinct based on scientific evidence. A listing triggers legal protections and recovery planning.
Figure 01

Sightings cluster along the coasts

Each dot is a recorded sighting of an EPBC-listed threatened species, coloured by taxonomic group (not by how severe its listing is, which the later charts explore). Click a group in the legend to isolate it.

SourceAtlas of Living Australia

The eastern seaboard and south-west corner light up, where high biodiversity and high survey effort overlap.

Mapping where these species occur is only half the picture. The dots reveal geographic clustering, but they say nothing about what kinds of life are most at risk. Breaking the same listed species down by taxonomic group exposes a striking imbalance, one that shapes the rest of this story.

Plants dominate the threatened list

SourceALA Species Checklist 2026

Biodiversity

Uniquely Australian

87% of Australia's mammals, 93% of reptiles, and 45% of birds are found nowhere else on Earth (Chapman 2009, DCCEEW).

The gap

Plants are under-studied

Despite making up 69% of listings, threatened plants receive far less public attention and funding than charismatic animals.

Survey bias

We find what we look for

Coastal regions show more sightings partly because that's where researchers are. Remote inland species may be under-recorded.

02   Burden
02Where It Hurts

How deep does it go?

More than 2,100 species now carry an EPBC threat category. The damage is not spread evenly. Some states shoulder far more, and certain taxonomic groups face disproportionate risk.

Figure 03

Density tells a different story than raw counts

Threatened species per 1,000 km² by state. Darker regions carry a heavier burden relative to their size.

SourceALA Checklist + CAPAD 2024

The Australian Capital Territory looks darkest (30.1 per 1,000 km²), but this is mostly an artifact of its tiny size: it has 71 listed species spread over a very small area, so the per-area figure inflates. Among the larger states, Tasmania (3.4) and Victoria (1.6) carry the heaviest density.

But density only tells part of the story. Normalising by area penalises large states that also hold vast numbers of threatened species in absolute terms. The next two charts break down the raw numbers, first by state and threat category, then by taxonomic group and geography, to show where the weight truly falls.

Figure 04

New South Wales and Queensland bear the heaviest load

Each bar stacks threat categories. Most severe sit at the baseline.

SourceALA Checklist 2026

New South Wales carries by far the heaviest load with over 750 listed species, followed by Western Australia and Queensland, each above 600.

The stacked bars show totals, but they hide which taxonomic groups drive each state's numbers. The heatmap below cross-references every group with every state, revealing that the story is overwhelmingly about plants, particularly in Queensland, New South Wales, and Western Australia.

Figure 05

Where groups meet geography

Colour is scaled within each state, not across the whole chart. Every cell is shaded by how it compares to that state's most threatened group, so the darkest cell sits at 100% and a cell at 50% means that group is half the size of the state's worst-hit one. Shades are not a share of the state's total, so they do not add up to 100% down a column. Numbers show the actual species count; hover any cell for its percentage.

SourceALA Checklist 2026

In six of the eight states, plants are the most-threatened group, the darkest cell in the column. Only the tiny Australian Capital Territory (where birds lead) and the Northern Territory (mammals) break the pattern.

Density matters

Normalising by area reveals a different picture. The Australian Capital Territory's tiny size concentrates its 71 listed species into the highest density figure, though this largely reflects its small area rather than exceptional biodiversity.

The takeaway: raw counts, density, and group breakdowns tell three different stories. New South Wales and Queensland carry the heaviest absolute burden, the Australian Capital Territory only leads once you correct for area, and across almost every state the crisis is overwhelmingly a plant crisis. Which measure you choose changes which state looks worst, so all three views are needed to see the full picture.

03   Trajectory
03The Direction of Travel

Is it getting worse?

Conservation is a race against time. These charts track how the crisis has evolved: which families carry the heaviest burden, how listing rates have shifted, and whether recent years mark an acceleration.

Figure 06

Orchids are the most threatened family

241 orchid species are listed, more than any other plant or animal family. Colour shows the taxonomic group.

SourceALA Checklist 2026

The top 5 families are all plants. The first animal family, Parastacidae (freshwater crayfish), appears at #7.

Knowing which families are most at risk tells us where to look. But understanding the pace of change tells us how urgently we need to act. The tree ring chart below shows how many species were added to the EPBC list each year since 2001. Like the growth rings of a tree, each slice records a year of accumulated threat.

Figure 07

Reading the rings of the crisis

Each wedge is one year, running clockwise from 2001 (top) to 2025; how far it reaches from the centre is the number of species added that year. There is no legend because angle shows the year and length shows the count, hover any wedge for its exact year and figure. The record year, 2023, is picked out in deep rust.

SourceAustralia's Environment 2025 (TERN/ANU)

One slice dwarfs the rest: 2023, the record year, with 130 additions, more than four times the long-run average of 31 per year.

The family-level breakdown reveals a pattern hidden by the aggregate numbers: the crisis is not evenly distributed across the tree of life. Orchids alone account for more threatened species than any entire animal group: there are more threatened orchids than threatened birds. Meanwhile, the temporal data shows that listings have accelerated sharply since the Black Summer bushfires of 2019 to 2020.

A single plant family, orchids, outnumbers every threatened bird in Australia, the largest animal group.Figure 06

Figure 08

Then vs. now

Additions 2000–2012 extend left; 2013–2025 extend right. Which categories accelerated?

SourceAustralia's Environment 2025 (TERN/ANU)

Critically Endangered listings more than doubled in the second period (291 versus 130), so the crisis is concentrating at the sharp end.

Those figures count new additions in two windows. But the crisis didn't arrive in two jumps. It built year on year. The line chart below lets the whole quarter-century unfold at once: each line is a threat category, its height the number of species carrying that status, with one line climbing far faster than the others.

Figure 09

One line bends sharply upward

Each line tracks a threat category from 2000 to 2025. Three lines rise gently; the Critically Endangered line (in rust) climbs far steeper than the rest. Hover any point to read its count.

SourceAustralia's Environment 2025 (TERN/ANU)

Critically Endangered listings rose more than thirtyfold, from just 13 in 2000 to 434 in 2025, with the steepest acceleration following the 2019–20 bushfires. No other category grew anywhere near as fast.

Key finding

CE is accelerating

Critically Endangered listings grew more than twice as fast in 2013–2025 as in the first half of the EPBC era.

Orchids at risk

241 species and counting

Orchidaceae alone accounts for around 11% of the listed species, more than any single animal group, and more than all threatened birds.

The 2023 spike

Record year: 130 additions

2023 saw the most species added in a single year, driven by post-bushfire reassessments and a wave of new invertebrate and freshwater crayfish listings.

The takeaway: the trajectory is not just upward, it is steepening. A single plant family, orchids, now carries more listings than every threatened bird combined, and the Critically Endangered count has climbed more than thirtyfold since 2000, from 13 to 434. The record year of 2023 and the sharp turn after the 2019–20 bushfires show the crisis is not only growing but concentrating at its most severe end.

04   Response
04Conservation in Action

What's being done?

Australia's protected area network now covers about 22.6% of the continent. But coverage is unevenly distributed, and the international 30% target remains out of reach for most states.

Figure 10

Where the protected land actually is

Each circle is a state, sized by its total terrestrial protected area (millions of hectares). Circle area is proportional to the value, not radius.

SourceCAPAD 2024 (DCCEEW)

Western Australia alone holds 45% of Australia's protected land, and the three arid western and central jurisdictions (Western Australia, the Northern Territory, and South Australia) together hold over 80%, far from the biodiverse but heavily developed east coast.

Size alone does not equal effectiveness. A large desert reserve protects different species than a small coastal fragment. The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework set a target of 30% land protection by 2030. The chart below shows how each state measures against that benchmark, revealing stark differences in conservation ambition.

Queensland carries just over 600 threatened species, yet protects the smallest share of its land of any state.Figures 04 & 11

Figure 11

Are we on track? Most states fall short.

Each whorl is a state, spiralling outward from least protected (innermost, palest) to most protected (outermost, deepest). Arc length shows the share of land protected; a longer arc means more of that state is safeguarded. The 30% mark is the target set by the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, the global "30 by 30" pledge to protect 30% of land and sea by 2030. Scientists treat roughly a third as the minimum needed to slow biodiversity loss and keep ecosystems functioning, which is why it is the line each state is measured against.

Queensland 9.4% New South Wales 10.4% Victoria 17.7% Northern Territory 24.6% South Australia 30.4% Western Australia 31.1% Tasmania 42.8% Australian Capital Territory 55.9%
SourceCAPAD 2024 (DCCEEW)

Only the Australian Capital Territory (55.9%), Tasmania (42.8%), Western Australia (31.1%), and South Australia (30.4%) have met the 30% target. Queensland sits at just 9.4%.

Met target

4 of 8 states

The Australian Capital Territory, Tasmania, Western Australia, and South Australia have reached 30%, but these include vast arid reserves with less biodiversity per hectare.

Falling short

Queensland: 9.4% protected

Queensland has nearly the most threatened species of any state, yet the lowest protection rate in the country, a clear mismatch.

The 30% target

A global benchmark

The Kunming-Montreal framework calls for 30% of land protected by 2030. Australia sits at 22.6% nationally.

The takeaway: protection exists, but it is in the wrong places. Most of the protected estate sits in the arid west and centre, with Western Australia alone holding 45% of it, while the biodiverse, heavily developed east coast lags behind. Queensland is the clearest mismatch: among the highest threatened-species loads in the country, yet the lowest protection at 9.4%. Reaching 30% nationally will mean protecting land where the species actually are, not just where it is easiest to set aside.

Where this leaves us.

Twenty-five years of EPBC Act data tell a story that should trouble every Australian. The threatened species list has grown 54% since 2000. Critically Endangered listings have risen from just 13 in 2000 to 434 in 2025, more than a thirtyfold increase. The 2019 to 2020 Black Summer bushfires alone impacted over half of all species added since. Climate change is now among the most frequently cited threats for newly listed species.

But the data also shows where action works. States that invest in protected areas, like the Australian Capital Territory at 55.9% and Tasmania at 42.8%, demonstrate that the 30% target is achievable. Targeted conservation, including feral predator control, fenced havens, and habitat restoration, has helped stabilise some individual threatened mammal populations, even as the national trend remains downward.

The question is whether policy will match the pace of the crisis. With Queensland protecting just 9.4% of its land despite holding 608 threatened species, the mismatch between burden and response remains stark. Conservation is not a finished project. It is an ongoing commitment, and the data makes the case clearly.

The crisis

+54% in 25 years

The EPBC list has grown from 1,412 to 2,175 species since 2000. The rate of listings has not slowed.

The bright spot

Protection can work

Where conservation investment is sustained through predator control, habitat restoration, and protected areas, populations stabilise.

The gap

Burden ≠ response

The states with the most threatened species often have the lowest protection rates. Closing this gap is the central challenge.
